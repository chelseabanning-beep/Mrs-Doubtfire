# Relationship Moves

## Purpose

This document turns the theory foundation into practical moves the AI mentor can coach during roleplay. These are the behaviors teachers should practice.

The moves are grounded in:

- CASEL social-emotional learning competencies.
- Search Institute Developmental Relationships.
- Self-Determination Theory.
- PBIS / MTSS.
- Trauma-informed practice.

## Quick Reference

| Move | Use When | Teacher Line Pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Connect before correct | Student is dysregulated, embarrassed, or attention-seeking. | "I hear you. Right now I need..." |
| Private over public | Correction could embarrass the student. | "Quick check-in..." |
| Name the expectation | Student needs clarity. | "I need [specific behavior] now." |
| Offer a path back | Student needs re-entry after correction. | "Do [next step], then..." |
| Use bounded choices | Student needs agency within limits. | "You can choose A or B." |
| Regulate first | Intensity is rising. | "Take a breath. We can reset." |
| Redirect briefly | Instruction needs to continue. | "Pause that and track here." |
| Repair later | Relationship needs follow-up after the moment. | "I will check in after class." |
| Neutralize language | Teacher language risks labeling or blame. | Replace identity labels with behavior descriptions. |
| Escalate support | Situation exceeds classroom practice. | Follow school support/policy. |

## Move 1: Connect Before Correct

### What It Means

Start with a brief signal that the student still belongs, then state the boundary.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: relationship skills, social awareness.
- Developmental Relationships: express care and challenge growth.
- Self-Determination Theory: relatedness.
- Trauma-informed practice: psychological safety.

### Use When

- A student calls out.
- A student jokes to get peer attention.
- A student resists because they feel embarrassed.
- A student seems defensive before the teacher has finished speaking.

### Teacher Lines

```text
I want to hear your idea. Right now I need one voice, and I will come back to you after this example.
```

```text
I can see this is frustrating. Start with the first step, and I will check back in two minutes.
```

### Common Mistake

Being warm but never naming the expectation.

### Mentor Tip

```text
The connection helps. Now add a clear boundary so the student knows what to do next.
```

## Move 2: Private Over Public

### What It Means

Correct in a way that avoids making the student the center of attention.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: social awareness.
- Developmental Relationships: respect me, hold me accountable.
- Trauma-informed practice: safety and trust.
- PBIS: respectful response to unwanted behavior.

### Use When

- The behavior is visible to peers.
- The student may feel embarrassed.
- The teacher is tempted to make an example of the student.

### Teacher Lines

```text
Quick reset: I need your voice off while I explain this. I will come back to you in a minute.
```

```text
I am going to check in with you quietly while everyone starts the next problem.
```

### Common Mistake

Using the class as pressure: "Everyone is waiting for you."

### Mentor Tip

```text
Try removing the audience from the correction. A private or low-profile redirect protects dignity and usually reduces escalation.
```

## Move 3: Name The Expectation

### What It Means

Say what the student should do, not only what they should stop doing.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: responsible decision-making.
- PBIS: explicitly teach expected behavior.
- Self-Determination Theory: competence.

### Use When

- The teacher's first response is vague.
- The student may not know the replacement behavior.
- The teacher says "stop" without a next step.

### Teacher Lines

```text
Track the speaker and keep your comment until I ask for responses.
```

```text
Open to page twelve and write the first sentence. I will check your start in one minute.
```

### Common Mistake

Only saying "stop talking" or "be respectful."

### Mentor Tip

```text
Make the expectation observable. Tell the student what success looks like in the next ten seconds.
```

## Move 4: Offer A Path Back

### What It Means

After a correction, give the student a clear way to rejoin the class successfully.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: relationship skills, responsible decision-making.
- Developmental Relationships: provide support.
- Self-Determination Theory: competence and relatedness.

### Use When

- The student has disrupted but can re-engage.
- The teacher wants accountability without rejection.
- The student seems stuck after being corrected.

### Teacher Lines

```text
Reset with us now. When I ask for ideas, I will invite you back in.
```

```text
Start with number one. If you get stuck, circle it and keep moving.
```

### Common Mistake

Correcting behavior but leaving the student socially or academically stranded.

### Mentor Tip

```text
Good boundary. Add the re-entry step so the student knows how to return to learning.
```

## Move 5: Use Bounded Choices

### What It Means

Offer two acceptable options that preserve the teacher's boundary.

### Theory Links

- Self-Determination Theory: autonomy.
- Developmental Relationships: share power.
- Trauma-informed practice: voice and choice.

### Use When

- Student refuses to begin.
- Student wants control.
- Teacher can offer choice without compromising the expectation.

### Teacher Lines

```text
You can start with the first problem or the second problem. Choose one and work for three minutes.
```

```text
You can move to the side table or stay here with your voice off. Which will help you focus?
```

### Common Mistake

Offering fake choices or too many choices.

### Mentor Tip

```text
The choice works because both options meet the classroom goal. Keep choices limited and real.
```

## Move 6: Regulate First

### What It Means

Lower the emotional intensity before solving the issue.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: self-management.
- Trauma-informed practice: safety.
- Self-Determination Theory: relatedness.

### Use When

- The student is visibly upset.
- The teacher is frustrated.
- The interaction is turning into a power struggle.

### Teacher Lines

```text
Pause. We can reset this without arguing. Take a minute at your desk, then I will check in.
```

```text
I am going to give us both a second. Start the warm-up, and I will come back quietly.
```

### Common Mistake

Trying to win the argument in front of the class.

### Mentor Tip

```text
This is a regulation moment before it is a problem-solving moment. Lower the intensity, then return to the expectation.
```

## Move 7: Redirect Briefly

### What It Means

Use a short, clear correction that keeps instruction moving.

### Theory Links

- PBIS: proactive and instructional response.
- CASEL: self-management.
- Trauma-informed practice: predictability.

### Use When

- The behavior is minor.
- The lesson flow matters.
- The teacher is tempted to over-explain.

### Teacher Lines

```text
Pause the side conversation and track the example.
```

```text
Pencils down for ten seconds. Eyes here.
```

### Common Mistake

Turning a minor correction into a long lecture.

### Mentor Tip

```text
Keep the redirect brief. Long explanations can create more attention for the behavior.
```

## Move 8: Repair Later

### What It Means

Follow up after the moment to rebuild trust, clarify expectations, and listen.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: relationship skills.
- Developmental Relationships: listen, respect me, reflect on failures.
- Trauma-informed practice: trust and collaboration.

### Use When

- The moment was tense.
- The student complied but seemed hurt or angry.
- The teacher used a response they want to repair.

### Teacher Lines

```text
I want to check in after class. I care about how that landed, and I also need us to keep the lesson respectful.
```

```text
Earlier got tense. I want to reset with you and make a plan for tomorrow.
```

### Common Mistake

Assuming compliance means the relationship is fine.

### Mentor Tip

```text
The in-the-moment response protected learning. A short follow-up can protect the relationship.
```

## Move 9: Neutralize Language

### What It Means

Describe the behavior without labeling the student's character, identity, ability, or intent.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: social awareness.
- Trauma-informed practice: dignity and safety.
- PBIS: observable behavior.

### Use When

- Teacher says "rude," "lazy," "disrespectful," "defiant," or "attention-seeking."
- Teacher assumes intent.
- Teacher uses sarcasm or a personal label.

### Teacher Lines

```text
The side conversation is interrupting the directions. I need voices off while I explain the next step.
```

```text
The work is not started yet. Choose the first or second problem and begin there.
```

### Common Mistake

Naming the student as the problem instead of naming the behavior.

### Mentor Tip

```text
Shift from a label to an observable behavior. That keeps the correction fair and easier to act on.
```

## Move 10: Escalate Support

### What It Means

Recognize when the situation requires a human support process rather than a practice roleplay.

### Theory Links

- CASEL: responsible decision-making.
- PBIS / MTSS: tiered support.
- Trauma-informed practice: safety and policy alignment.

### Use When

- There is a safety concern.
- A student discloses harm or abuse.
- There is self-harm, violence, severe harassment, or a mandated reporting issue.
- The teacher needs school-specific policy guidance.

### Teacher Lines

```text
I am going to make sure we get the right support. I will follow our school process and connect with the appropriate adult right away.
```

```text
This is important, and I do not want you to handle it alone. I am going to involve the support team.
```

### Common Mistake

Trying to solve a safety or policy issue through casual classroom redirection.

### Mentor Tip

```text
This is outside a practice roleplay. Follow your school policy and involve the appropriate administrator, counselor, safety lead, or support team.
```

## Mentor Selection Rule

For each teacher reply, choose one primary move and one secondary move.

Example:

```text
Primary move: Name the expectation.
Secondary move: Offer a path back.
Mentor tip: "You named the behavior. Now give the student a path back: 'Pause the comment and track here. I will come back to your idea after this example.'"
```
